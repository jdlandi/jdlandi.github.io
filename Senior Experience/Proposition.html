Goal

Senior experience opens a lot of opportunities to continue learning with the flexibility to choose the path I want to follow.

Through this semester's senior experience, I want to explore crucial areas in mathematics that I haven't had the opportunity to do so. More specifically, fully completing my knowledge in Multivariable Calculus through Integration, and exploring the basics of Linear Algebra and Differential Equations, both subjects that I will have much more riguours classes on in the future and subjects I have yet to truly explore.

Furthermore, I want to truly begin immersing myself in cybersecurity. By following pathways known to teach me what I need, I will be able to take my knowledge to the next level, taking my learning to applications, rather than just reading. Different services allow for this, but labs and interactive options exist that I have been using and will continue to use through this senior experience project.

Alongside everything else, I plan on doing more activities related to web design, computer science teaching through being a teaching assistant, and continuing to find more opportunities to enrich my learning.

Why now

After taking an entire semester's worth of Calculus I at UChicago, I can say for certain that the experience I gained was amazing. It helped me really challenge myself and get the opportunity to take an extremely rigurous course at a prestigous school. However, I think that for this upcoming semester, instead of continouing the arduos process, taking upwards of 2 hours on transportation everyday and being late to my hardest class this year frequently, I want to switch over to a more regular senior experience styled learning.

Not only do I currently feel unprepared for my classes at Caltech, I want to really spend the majority of my time learning more of what I know I will need to learn now. Linear algebra is the core of so many amazing parts of mathematics, it is the field that allows for so much more, yet I do not know one bit. After talking with a few people, I came to the conclusion that learning even the basics of fields like these will benefit me a lot more now, than not knowing them and taking a more rigurous course at Caltech.

Either way, as it is just the end of the semester, this provides an amazing opportunity to start anew without feeling like it was a sudden change or in the middle of a school quarter. I know that my own personal skills will grow, and with what I have achieved already, I am confident in my own ability to pace myself and do what I require. After all, cybersecurity is a field where the learning never stops, what would I be if I couldn't learn by myself without a professor or teacher there to push me?

Resources

James Stewart. Calculus Early Transcendentals. Eleventh Edition.

I took the first portion of Multivariable Calculus through Northwestern's dual enrollment program and really enjoyed the class, despite it being virtual. I also noticed that I was really drawn to the style the textbook presents everything in, so I want to continue learning here. The reason as to why I haven't done Northwestern's dual enrollment again was due to missing the extremely early financial aid timeframe they offer, otherwise I would be $5,000 down for each class.

David C. Lay, Steven R. Lay, and Judi J. McDonald. Linear Algebra and Its Applications. Sixth Edition.

This is the textbook that I have chosen to begin learning linear algebra from. While it is a longer textbook, I did read the first bits of various one, looking at the kinds of problems they offered and how they formatted the lessons and found myself to like this one the best.

Regarding cybersecurity, there are quite a numerous of either cheap or free services online that I have already begun using and plan to continue to do so, but at a much more rapid pace. From free virtual machines to practice capture-the-flag-esque scenarios, to very well-made lessons with hands-on projects. Regardless, I know that I can find the best quality material and truly make the most from it.
