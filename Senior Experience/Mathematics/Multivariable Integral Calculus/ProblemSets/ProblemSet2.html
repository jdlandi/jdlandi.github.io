Problem 1

Suppose f(x,y) is continuous and nonnegative over a region R in the plane with a defined area A(R). if ∬Rf(x,y)dA=0, prove that f(x,y)=0 at every point (x,y)∈R.

The best way to go about this problem is to look at the Riemann Sum definition. Without needing to write it all out, it relies on the idea of taking a random point on a random square in R. We can prove this with a simply proof by contradiction.

Assume that f(x,y)≠0 on some point (a,b). Because of the nature of Riemann sums, as we take the limit we will eventually be adding this point to the "volume." However, because our final volume is 0, there must exist another point (m,n), where f(m,n)=−f(a,b) because when you add these two points, you will get a total volume equal to 0. [1]

However, we are told that f is non-negative, so therefore this contradicts the principle that the area of f over the region R is equal to 0, as there cannot be a negative value for f. This means that our initial assumption, there exists a value where f(a,b)≠0 is wrong, and therefore f(a,b) must be 0 throughout the region R where it's volume is 0.

The second Idea is of the average value. The volume of an object, or function, divided by the area for which the volume is taken from, the region, will give the average value of said function, or the equivalent average height.

The average value on the disk is the same as taking the polar integral all around the center of the disk (in this case it is the origin) and the values from 0 to the radius of the disk, a in our case. Or:
